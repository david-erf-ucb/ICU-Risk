Project Summary

Why we built Vital Connect —
and what we learned.

A plain-language overview of the clinical problem, the engineering choices, and the two things we would tell every clinical ML team to remember.

"That's not drama. That's Tuesday in any ICU in the country."

The problem we started with

The Pitt is a medical drama set in a Pittsburgh emergency department. Every episode covers one hour of a single 15-hour shift. ICU clinicians are making life-or-death calls on patients they have known for minutes, based on vitals that are already old — with no system telling them who is about to deteriorate next.

That is not fiction. In real ICUs right now, the primary tool for tracking patient status is a manually updated spreadsheet. By the time a number is typed in, it is already old. If the unit is short-staffed — which the data confirms is the norm, not the exception — it may not be updated at all.

Sepsis affects 37% of ICU patients and is responsible for over 300,000 US deaths per year. Late identification adds an average of $33,000 to the cost of a single stay. The clinical need is clear and the commercial case is undeniable.

What Vital Connect does

Vital Connect scores every patient in an ICU simultaneously for both sepsis and mortality risk, updating every 5 seconds. No one types anything. The dashboard surfaces two views: a ranked table for a clinician moving between beds on the floor, and a 24-hour risk trajectory for a clinician with a moment to sit down at a desk.

The model is trained on MIMIC-IV and eICU — the gold standard public ICU datasets, combined via the METRE pipeline. Together they represent over 100,000 ICU stays across more than 200 hospitals.

The two things we learned

After months of ablation studies, architecture experiments, and honest evaluation, two lessons stood out above everything else.

  • The pipeline mattered more than the model. The single biggest performance jump came not from switching to a deeper architecture. It came from z-score normalisation — getting features onto comparable scales. In clinical ML, how you prepare the data is the most consequential decision you make.
  • Catching the right patients is harder than it looks. A model that never predicts a single death achieves 85% accuracy on mortality. That sounds impressive until you realise it is clinically useless. AUROC told us we had a good ranking model. AUPRC told the truth. For rare outcomes, the metric you choose determines whether you build something useful or something that just looks good on a leaderboard.

What comes next

Vital Connect is a meaningful step — not a finished product. Three things sit between the prototype and a deployable clinical tool: EHR integration via HL7 FHIR R4 to replace the simulation with live hospital data; a clinician feedback loop that turns every alert override into a training signal; and prospective shadow validation — running the model live, invisible to clinicians, to measure whether earlier predictions would actually have changed care.

The architecture generalises. Swap the target from mortality to AKI, readmission, or ward deterioration and the pipeline is identical. Trained weights require local calibration before deployment at a new institution. More real-world data from diverse hospital populations is the single highest-leverage investment for making this work at scale.

Architecture · Modeling

Why we chose LSTM over Transformers for ICU time-series

February 6, 2026 · David Erf

In 2026, defaulting to a Transformer architecture for any sequence task feels almost automatic. Attention is all you need, the paper said, and the field largely agreed. So when we started scoping the Vital Connect model, the assumption in the room was that we would use a Transformer variant.

We didn't. Here is why.

The ICU data problem

ICU time-series data has a specific structure that does not play to Transformer strengths. The sequences are short — 24 hourly timesteps. Transformers benefit most from long sequences where global attention patterns matter. At 24 steps, the quadratic attention cost buys you very little that a well-designed LSTM cannot match.

More importantly, ICU deterioration is a fundamentally sequential process. A patient's lactate at hour 18 is causally downstream of their lactate at hour 6. LSTM's inductive bias toward sequential processing is not a limitation here — it is a feature.

The attention layer we did add

We added a temporal attention mechanism on top of the LSTM outputs — not self-attention across all positions, but a learned weighting that tells the model which timesteps to emphasise when making its final prediction. This captures the key benefit of attention (dynamic weighting of relevant history) without the overhead of full Transformer machinery.

mortality_model: type: LSTM layers: 2 hidden_size: 128 attention: temporal params: ~350K metre_baseline: type: LSTM params: ~5.7M

The result: our ~350K parameter model outperforms the 5.7M parameter METRE-style baseline on AUPRC. Scale was never the answer. Architecture fit was.

The missingness problem: why a missing lab result is not a zero

February 19, 2026 · David Erf

The naive approach to missing values in a neural network is to fill them with zero. It is fast, simple, and wrong — at least for clinical data.

What zero-filling actually tells the model

When you fill a missing lactate with 0.0, you are telling the model that the patient's lactate was zero. A lactate of zero is physiologically impossible and represents extreme cellular dysfunction. You have just corrupted your training data in a way that the model will eventually learn to ignore — but only imperfectly.

The deeper problem is that zero-filling destroys the difference between two very different clinical situations: a patient whose lactate was measured and came back normal, and a patient whose lactate was never drawn. Those are not the same thing. A clinician who does not draw a lactate is making a decision. That decision carries information.

Missingness as a feature

We encode missingness explicitly. For every feature that could be missing, we add a corresponding binary indicator: was this value present at this timestep? The model can now learn that a lactate that goes unmeasured for 8 hours while the patient is deteriorating is a different signal than a lactate that goes unmeasured because the patient is stable.

This added meaningful lift to AUPRC in the ablation — not the largest single jump (that was z-score normalisation), but consistent and reliable improvement across multiple runs.

Evaluation · Metrics

AUROC lied to us. AUPRC told the truth.

March 4, 2026 · David Erf & Bilal Paracha

Mortality affects 15% of our ICU patients. That means 85% of patients survive. A model that predicts survival for every single patient — without learning anything — achieves 85% accuracy and a respectable-looking AUROC.

We know this. Every clinical ML practitioner knows this. And yet when our first model came back with an AUROC of 0.82, the room felt good. We should have looked at AUPRC first.

What AUROC actually measures

AUROC measures whether the model ranks positive cases above negative cases. At 85% negative prevalence, there are six survivors for every death in the dataset. AUROC is heavily influenced by how well the model handles that large majority class. A model can look strong on AUROC while completely failing to find the borderline patients who matter most clinically.

Why AUPRC is the honest metric

AUPRC ignores true negatives. It only evaluates performance on the positive class — the deaths. With 15% positive rate, a random classifier achieves an AUPRC of 0.15. Every point above that represents genuine predictive ability on the class that matters. Our final model achieves 0.55 AUPRC. That is not a stunning number. It is an honest one.

The clinical framing that helped us: what percentage of deaths does the model catch at a false alarm rate the ward can live with? At 10% false positive rate, we catch approximately two-thirds of deaths. That is the number a clinical team actually has to work with.

Design · Dashboard

Designing a dashboard for a clinician under pressure

March 17, 2026 · Bilal Paracha

The first version of the Vital Connect dashboard was built by an engineer. It was technically correct and clinically unusable. Every design decision since then has been made by asking one question: what does an ICU clinician need in the 90 seconds they have between patients?

Two modes of use

A clinician interacts with a risk dashboard in two distinct contexts. On the floor, moving between beds, they need a ranked list they can scan in under 10 seconds without touching a keyboard. At a desk, with a minute to sit down, they want to understand why a specific patient is flagged and what the trajectory looks like over the last 24 hours.

The two-column layout directly mirrors this split. Left column: the ranked table, colour-coded by risk level, optimised for scan speed. Right column: the patient detail panel, trajectory chart, vitals, and clinical context. Everything in the right column is hidden until a patient is selected.

Colour as clinical language

Red is critical. Orange is high. Amber is alert. Green is monitor. These are not arbitrary choices — they map directly to the colour language most ICU nurses and physicians already use on whiteboards and handover sheets. A new system that requires learning new colour semantics is a system that will be ignored.

The 80/20 weight decision

Weighting mortality at 80% and sepsis at 20% in the combined risk score was initially controversial. The argument for equal weighting was intuitive. The argument for 80/20 was clinical: mortality risk integrates the full severity picture — it incorporates sepsis, organ dysfunction, haemodynamic instability, and frailty simultaneously. Sepsis alone, while time-sensitive, is often reversible. The combined score should reflect urgency, and urgency tracks mortality.

Pipeline · Ablation

Preprocessing was the model. The ablation proved it.

March 28, 2026 · David Erf

We ran a cumulative ablation study — starting from the METRE-style baseline with zero-filling and standard cross-entropy, then adding one component at a time and measuring AUPRC at each step.

We expected the attention mechanism to produce the largest jump. We expected focal loss to make a meaningful difference. We did not expect z-score normalisation to outperform both of them combined.

What the ablation showed

Getting features onto comparable scales — normalising each feature to zero mean and unit variance — produced the single largest single-step AUPRC improvement in the entire study. Larger than adding attention. Larger than switching from cross-entropy to focal loss. Larger than any architectural change we tried.

The intuition in retrospect is obvious: a network that receives heart rate in units of beats per minute alongside Glasgow Coma Scale scores of 3–15 alongside lactate in mmol/L is solving a numerics problem before it can solve a clinical one. Z-score normalisation removes that problem. Everything downstream improves.

What this means for clinical ML

The lesson generalises. Before you argue about attention depth, hidden state size, or loss function, make sure your input pipeline is doing the right thing. In clinical data — heterogeneous units, wildly different scales, mix of continuous and binary features — preprocessing is not a detail. It is the work.

Product · Roadmap

From Colab notebook to clinical product: the three-step roadmap

April 8, 2026 · Bilal Paracha

Vital Connect works. The model performs. The dashboard is clean and responsive. And it runs on a Google Colab notebook with a synthetic JSON file simulating patient data. The gap between that and something a hospital will trust with real patients is significant — and it is worth being honest about what it would actually take to close it.

Step 1: EHR integration via FHIR

The simulation cell that writes to synthetic_patients.json needs to be replaced with a live data listener. Most major hospital systems — Epic, Cerner, Meditech — now expose HL7 FHIR R4 endpoints as a result of US regulatory requirements. A FHIR listener that subscribes to Observation and MedicationAdministration resources and feeds them into the scoring pipeline would turn the Colab demo into a deployable product in a single engineering sprint.

This is a 3–6 month engineering project, not a research problem. The architecture is already correct. The work is integration, not invention.

Step 2: Clinician feedback loop

Every time a clinician dismisses an alert or escalates a patient the model ranked low, that is a labelled training example. A lightweight capture mechanism embedded in the dashboard, combined with a quarterly retraining pipeline, would allow Vital Connect to continuously learn from the institution it is deployed in. This closes the distribution gap between MIMIC-IV and the live hospital population — a gap that will otherwise grow over time as patient demographics, protocols, and documentation practices evolve.

Step 3: Prospective shadow validation

Before any deployment claim can be made in good conscience, the model needs to run live — invisible to clinicians — and demonstrate that its predictions would have triggered earlier intervention in measurable cases. This is the step that closes the gap between a research result and clinical trust. It is also the hardest step, requiring institutional partnership, IRB approval, and sustained follow-through. But it is not optional.
